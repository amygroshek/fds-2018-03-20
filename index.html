Gilbert & Gubar

Gilbert and Gubar argued that the "maddened doubles" in texts by women writers of the nineteenth and twentieth century "function as social surrogates," projecting women writers' anxiety of authorship in a male-dominated literary tradition (Donaldson, 99)

Feminism & Madness

  1. Contemporary to the development of the social model of disability
  2. Emphasis on authorship, makes sense for literary criticism... extension to disability studies...
  3. Presumed whiteness, cis- and straight-ness, and class position of the "mad" women in question

Donaldson

A feminist disability studies reading that stresses the connections between madness and physiognomy, between the mind and body, provides us with an alternate way of conceptualizing madness in Jane Eyre... to complicate the current constructions of madness within feminist theory. (102)

Donaldson

  • Phrenology: Assessment (often racialized) of human capacity based on size & shape of the skull. Cesare Lombroso
  • Physiognomy: The assessment of character from outward appearance, particularly the face. (Especially prevalent in Jane Eyre.)

"The Yellow Wallpaper"

The feminist reading of the novel is somewhat at odds with the disability studies or crip reading of the novel... Because the feminist reading of the novel needs to invest in the medical model of disability in order to critique patriarchal society.

Impairment and Disability

A feminist disabilty studies reading... demands closer attention to physical bodies and to the theories of embodiment that structure the novel (110)

In particular, physiognomy in the 19th century... though Donaldson argues that it carries over into contemporary studies in neuroscience: "all share a basic understanding of the brain as a territory to be mapped" (110)

Donaldson on the Medical Model

Citing Simi Linton...

[M]edical definitions of disability in the past have functioned to keep disabilty "within the purview of the medical establishment, to keep it a personal matter and 'treat' the condition and the person with the condition rather than the 'treating' the social processes and policies that constrict disabled people's lives (110)
